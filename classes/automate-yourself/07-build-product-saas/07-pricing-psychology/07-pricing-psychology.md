# Pricing Psychology and Courage: Why Higher Prices Win

## Learning Objective

Understand why you shouldn't fear raising prices, how losing customers can increase margins, and the psychology behind pricing decisions that maximize revenue.

## Main Content

**The Pricing Fear:**

Most founders are terrified of pricing. They worry:
- "What if nobody buys at this price?"
- "What if I lose customers?"
- "What if competitors are cheaper?"
- "What if I'm wrong?"

**Reality**: This fear kills more startups than overpricing. You're more likely to fail from pricing too low than pricing too high.

**The Math That Changes Everything:**

**Scenario: You have 100 customers at $50/month**

**Current State**:
- Revenue: $5,000/month
- Customers: 100
- Margin (assuming 70%): $3,500/month

**Raise price to $75/month. Lose 20% of customers (20 customers quit).**

**New State**:
- Revenue: $6,000/month (80 customers × $75)
- Customers: 80
- Margin: $4,200/month (70% of $6,000)

**Result**: You lost 20 customers but made $700/month MORE profit. You need 20% fewer customers to serve. You're more profitable.

**The Price Increase Paradox:**

When you raise prices:
- You lose some customers (usually the worst ones)
- You gain more revenue from remaining customers
- You increase margins
- You attract better customers
- You reduce support burden

**Why Losing Customers Can Be Good:**

**The Customers You Lose Are Usually**:
- Price-sensitive (always looking for discounts)
- High-maintenance (complain about everything)
- Low-value (don't use many features)
- Likely to churn anyway (not engaged)

**The Customers You Keep Are Usually**:
- Value-focused (understand what you're worth)
- Engaged (actually use your product)
- Loyal (won't leave for small price differences)
- Advocates (refer others)

**The Price Elasticity Myth:**

Most founders think: "If I raise prices 20%, I'll lose 20% of customers."

**Reality**: Price elasticity is usually much lower than you think. Most customers:
- Don't notice small price increases (10-20%)
- Won't leave for reasonable increases
- Value convenience over price
- Are already invested in your product

**When You SHOULD Raise Prices:**

**1. High Demand, Low Supply**
- You have more customers than you can handle
- You're turning people away
- You have a waitlist

**2. Adding Value**
- You've launched new features
- You've improved service
- You've grown the product

**3. Low Churn**
- Customers aren't leaving
- High retention rate
- Low support burden

**4. Competitors Charge More**
- You're underpriced in the market
- You're leaving money on the table
- You can justify higher price

**5. You're Growing Too Slowly**
- Can't afford to hire
- Can't afford marketing
- Can't invest in product

**The Psychology of Price Increases:**

**Grandfathering (Keep Old Customers at Old Price)**:
- **Pros**: No immediate churn, happy existing customers
- **Cons**: Two-tier pricing, reduced revenue from best customers
- **When to Use**: Early stage, small customer base, testing

**Announcement (Give Notice, Apply to All)**:
- **Pros**: Fair, maximizes revenue, simple pricing
- **Cons**: Some churn, requires courage
- **When to Use**: Established product, confident in value

**Value-Add (Raise Price with New Features)**:
- **Pros**: Easier to justify, adds value, reduces pushback
- **Cons**: Have to deliver on promises
- **When to Use**: Always—combine price increases with value

**The Price Increase Email Template:**

**Subject**: We're raising prices (and here's why)

**Body**:

"We're raising prices on [Date] because:
- We've added [New Features]
- We've improved [Service/Support]
- We're investing in [Growth/Product]

**What this means for you**:
- Existing customers: Grandfathered at current price until [Date]
- New customers: New pricing effective [Date]
- Your price: [Current Price] (locked in until [Date])

Thank you for being an early supporter. We're building something special, and this helps us do it."

**Common Pricing Mistakes:**

**1. Pricing Too Low "To Get Customers"**
- Problem: Can't afford to grow, attracts wrong customers, signals low quality
- Solution: Price higher from the start. You can always discount.

**2. Never Raising Prices**
- Problem: Leaving money on the table, can't invest in growth
- Solution: Raise prices annually or when you add value.

**3. Being Afraid of Pushback**
- Problem: Underpricing, can't scale
- Solution: Expect pushback. It's normal. Most customers will stay.

**4. Competing on Price**
- Problem: Race to bottom, no differentiation
- Solution: Compete on value, not price. Price is a signal of quality.

**5. Not Testing Price Points**
- Problem: Assuming you know the right price
- Solution: Test different prices. You don't know until you test.

**The Courage to Price Correctly:**

**Why Founders Undervalue Their Product:**

**1. Imposter Syndrome**
- "Who am I to charge this much?"
- Reality: If you're solving a problem, you're worth it.

**2. Comparison Trap**
- "Competitors charge less"
- Reality: You might be better. Price accordingly.

**3. Fear of Rejection**
- "What if they say no?"
- Reality: Some will say no. That's fine. Focus on those who say yes.

**4. Underestimating Value**
- "I'm just a small startup"
- Reality: Value delivered is what matters, not company size.

**5. Wanting to Be Liked**
- "I want everyone to afford it"
- Reality: You can't help anyone if you go out of business.

**The Price-Value Relationship:**

**Higher Prices Signal**:
- Quality
- Value
- Exclusivity
- Professionalism
- Investment worth

**Lower Prices Signal**:
- Cheap
- Low quality
- Commodity
- Desperation
- Lack of confidence

**What Your Customers Think:**

**When You Price Too Low**:
- "Is this a scam?"
- "What's wrong with it?"
- "Why is it so cheap?"
- "Is this legitimate?"

**When You Price Correctly**:
- "This must be valuable"
- "They're confident in their product"
- "This is worth the investment"
- "I'm getting quality"

**The $10k MRR Math at Different Prices:**

**At $50/month**:
- Need: 200 customers
- With 5% churn: ~220 customers to maintain
- Support burden: High (more customers)

**At $100/month**:
- Need: 100 customers
- With 5% churn: ~110 customers to maintain
- Support burden: Medium

**At $200/month**:
- Need: 50 customers
- With 5% churn: ~55 customers to maintain
- Support burden: Low

**Higher prices = Fewer customers = Less support = More profit**

**Real-World Examples:**

**Example 1: SaaS Tool**
- Started: $29/month
- Problem: Can't afford to grow, high churn from low-value customers
- Raised to: $79/month
- Result: Lost 30% of customers, gained 40% revenue, better customers, profitable

**Example 2: Consulting Service**
- Started: $50/hour
- Problem: Can't afford to hire, always booked, no time for growth
- Raised to: $150/hour
- Result: Lost 20% of clients, made 80% more revenue, better clients, scalable

**Example 3: Digital Product**
- Started: $99 one-time
- Problem: Can't afford to create new products, no recurring revenue
- Switched to: $49/month subscription
- Result: Lost 40% of customers, gained 200% revenue, sustainable business

**The Price Testing Framework:**

**Step 1: Identify Your Current Price**
- What are you charging now?
- What's your margin?
- What's your churn?

**Step 2: Calculate Target Price**
- What's your value-based price?
- What do competitors charge?
- What would you pay?

**Step 3: Test Incrementally**
- Week 1-2: Test 10% higher with new customers
- Week 3-4: Test 20% higher with new customers
- Week 5-6: Test 30% higher with new customers
- Measure: Conversion rate, revenue, customer quality

**Step 4: Analyze Results**
- Did conversion drop significantly?
- Did revenue increase?
- Are customers better quality?
- Is support burden lower?

**Step 5: Make Decision**
- If revenue increased: Raise prices for all
- If revenue decreased: Lower back or test different price
- If mixed: Test more, find optimal price

**The Bottom Line:**

**Price Higher Than You Think**: You're probably underpricing. Most founders are.

**Raise Prices Regularly**: Annual increases or when you add value. Don't be afraid.

**Losing Customers Is OK**: Price-sensitive customers aren't your ideal customers. Losing them often increases margins.

**Test Constantly**: You don't know the right price until you test. Test different prices.

**Have Courage**: Pricing requires courage. Expect pushback. Most customers will stay. Focus on those who value your product.

## Key Takeaways

- Losing customers when raising prices often increases margins—you make more with fewer customers
- The customers you lose are usually the worst ones (price-sensitive, high-maintenance, low-value)
- Price elasticity is usually lower than you think—most customers won't leave for reasonable increases
- Higher prices signal quality, value, and professionalism—lower prices signal cheap and low quality
- For $10k MRR: Higher prices = fewer customers = less support = more profit
- Test prices constantly—you don't know the right price until you test
- Have courage—expect pushback, but most customers will stay at higher prices
- Price for profitability from the start—you can always discount, but raising is harder
