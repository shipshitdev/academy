## Main Content

Bitcoin was created in 2009 by an anonymous person (or group) using the name Satoshi Nakamoto. It's the first cryptocurrency and remains the largest by market value.

**What is Bitcoin?**

Bitcoin is a decentralized digital currency that enables peer-to-peer transactions without banks or governments. It's often called "digital gold" because, like gold, it has limited supply (21 million coins maximum) and is seen as a store of value.

**How Bitcoin Works**:

**The Network**: Thousands of computers worldwide run Bitcoin software, maintaining the network and verifying transactions.

**Mining**: Specialized computers (miners) solve complex math problems to verify transactions. Winners receive newly created Bitcoin as reward. This process secures the network.

**Transactions**: When you send Bitcoin, miners verify you own it and have sufficient balance. Once verified, it's recorded on the blockchain permanently.

**Supply**: New Bitcoin is created through mining, but the rate decreases over time. Total supply is capped at 21 million coins (expected around year 2140).

**Why Bitcoin Matters**:

**Store of Value**: Many see Bitcoin as a hedge against inflation and currency devaluation, similar to gold.

**Decentralization**: No single entity controls Bitcoin. It's resistant to censorship and government interference.

**Scarcity**: Limited supply creates scarcity (unlike fiat money that can be printed endlessly).

**Global Access**: Anyone with internet can use Bitcoin, regardless of location or banking access.

**Transparency**: All transactions are public (though not always easily traceable to individuals).

**How to Get Bitcoin**:

**Exchanges**: Buy Bitcoin on cryptocurrency exchanges (Coinbase, Binance, Kraken, etc.) using traditional money.

**Peer-to-Peer**: Buy directly from individuals using platforms like LocalBitcoins (requires more caution).

**Mining**: Technically possible but expensive and requires specialized equipment.

**Bitcoin ATMs**: Physical machines that sell Bitcoin (usually higher fees).

**Accepting as Payment**: If you sell goods/services, accept Bitcoin as payment.

**Understanding Bitcoin Price**:

**Volatility**: Bitcoin's price can change dramatically. It's not uncommon to see 10-20% moves in a day.

**Factors Affecting Price**:
- Adoption and mainstream acceptance
- Regulatory news (government decisions)
- Market sentiment and media coverage
- Supply and demand
- Major events (halvings, institutional adoption)

**Price History**: Bitcoin has experienced massive bull runs and brutal bear markets. Past performance doesn't guarantee future results.

**Bitcoin Storage**:

**Hot Wallets**: Software wallets connected to internet (convenient but less secure). Examples: Coinbase, Exodus, Electrum.

**Cold Wallets**: Hardware devices or paper wallets offline (more secure for large amounts). Examples: Ledger, Trezor.

**Exchange Storage**: Leaving Bitcoin on exchanges (convenient but you don't control private keys—higher risk).

**Best Practice**: Use exchanges to buy, move to personal wallet for long-term storage.

**Bitcoin vs. Other Cryptocurrencies**:

**Bitcoin Advantages**:
- First mover advantage (most recognized)
- Largest market cap and liquidity
- Most secure network (most mining power)
- Widest adoption and acceptance

**Bitcoin Limitations**:
- Slower transactions than some newer cryptocurrencies
- Higher fees during busy periods
- Less programmable than platforms like Ethereum

**Common Bitcoin Concepts**:

**Satoshi**: Smallest unit of Bitcoin (1 Bitcoin = 100 million Satoshis). Named after creator Satoshi Nakamoto.

**Halving**: Event every 4 years where mining rewards are cut in half. Reduces new supply, historically correlated with price increases.

**Confirmation**: Each block added after your transaction increases security. Most accept 1-6 confirmations as final.

**Lightning Network**: Second-layer solution enabling faster, cheaper Bitcoin transactions.

**Important Considerations**:

**Not Anonymous**: Bitcoin is pseudonymous. Transactions are public and can sometimes be traced.

**Irreversible**: Once sent, Bitcoin transactions can't be reversed. Send to wrong address = lost forever.

**Regulatory Uncertainty**: Governments are still figuring out how to regulate Bitcoin. Regulations can affect price and usage.

**Energy Consumption**: Bitcoin mining uses significant electricity. This is a criticism and ongoing area of improvement.

## Key Takeaways
- Bitcoin is the first and largest cryptocurrency, often called "digital gold"
- Limited supply (21 million), decentralized, and secured by mining
- Highly volatile but seen as store of value and hedge against inflation
- Store Bitcoin in personal wallets for security, not on exchanges long-term
- Bitcoin is pseudonymous (not fully anonymous) and transactions are irreversible
