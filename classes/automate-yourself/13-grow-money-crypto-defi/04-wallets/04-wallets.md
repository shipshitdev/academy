## Main Content

Cryptocurrency security is critical. Unlike traditional banking, crypto transactions are irreversible. If you lose access or get hacked, your funds are likely gone forever. Here's how to protect yourself:

**Understanding Wallets**:

**What Wallets Actually Store**:
Wallets don't store cryptocurrency—they store private keys. Your crypto exists on the blockchain. Private keys prove ownership and allow you to send it.

**Private Key**: Secret code that proves you own the cryptocurrency. If someone gets it, they can steal your crypto. If you lose it, you lose access forever.

**Public Address**: Like a bank account number. You share this to receive payments. It's safe to share publicly.

**Types of Wallets**:

**Hot Wallets** (Connected to Internet):

**Exchange Wallets**:
- Stored on cryptocurrency exchanges
- Convenient for trading
- You don't control private keys (exchange does)
- Risk: Exchange can be hacked or go bankrupt
- **Best for**: Small amounts, active trading
- **Not for**: Long-term storage of large amounts

**Software Wallets**:
- Apps on your phone or computer
- You control private keys
- More secure than exchanges
- Still vulnerable to malware/phishing
- Examples: Exodus, Electrum, MetaMask
- **Best for**: Medium amounts, regular use

**Cold Wallets** (Offline):

**Hardware Wallets**:
- Physical devices (like USB drives)
- Private keys stored offline
- Most secure option for large amounts
- Cost: $50-200 for device
- Examples: Ledger, Trezor
- **Best for**: Long-term storage, significant amounts

**Paper Wallets**:
- Private keys printed on paper
- Completely offline
- Free but requires careful handling
- Vulnerable to physical damage/loss
- **Best for**: Very long-term storage (if done correctly)

**Security Best Practices**:

**1. Use Hardware Wallets for Large Amounts**
- If holding significant value, invest in hardware wallet
- Private keys never leave device
- Even if computer is hacked, crypto is safe

**2. Never Share Private Keys or Seed Phrases**
- Never share with anyone, ever
- Legitimate services never ask for them
- Scammers attempt to trick you

**3. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)**
- Use on all exchanges and wallets that support it
- Use authenticator app (not SMS if possible)
- Adds extra security layer

**4. Use Strong, Unique Passwords**
- Different password for each account
- Use password manager
- Long, random passwords

**5. Be Wary of Phishing**
- Double-check URLs (scammers use similar domains)
- Never click links in suspicious emails
- Always go directly to website
- Verify SSL certificates

**6. Keep Software Updated**
- Update wallet software regularly
- Update computer/phone operating systems
- Patches fix security vulnerabilities

**7. Backup Your Seed Phrase**
- Write down seed phrase (recovery words)
- Store in secure, offline location
- Multiple secure copies (fireproof safe)
- Never store digitally (screenshots, cloud, etc.)

**8. Start Small**
- Practice with small amounts first
- Learn security before storing large amounts
- Test recovery process

**Common Security Mistakes**:

**Storing on Exchanges Long-Term**: 
- Exchanges are prime targets for hackers
- Not your keys, not your crypto

**Sharing Private Keys**:
- No legitimate service needs your private key
- Scammers will ask for it

**Saving Seed Phrases Digitally**:
- Screenshots, cloud storage, emails are vulnerable
- Write on paper, store securely

**Using Public WiFi for Crypto**:
- Unsecured networks can be monitored
- Use VPN or avoid crypto activities on public WiFi

**Clicking Suspicious Links**:
- Phishing sites look legitimate
- Always verify URLs

**Hardware Wallet Setup**:

**Step 1**: Buy from official source (avoid third-party sellers)

**Step 2**: Initialize device, write down seed phrase

**Step 3**: Test recovery (reset device, restore from seed phrase)

**Step 4**: Transfer small amount, test sending

**Step 5**: Once confident, transfer larger amounts

**The "Not Your Keys, Not Your Crypto" Principle**:

If you don't control the private keys, you don't truly own the cryptocurrency. Exchanges can freeze accounts, go bankrupt, or be hacked. For true ownership, use wallets where you control keys.

**Balancing Security and Convenience**:

**Small Amounts**: Exchange or software wallet is fine for convenience.

**Medium Amounts**: Software wallet with good security practices.

**Large Amounts**: Hardware wallet is essential.

**Emergency Access**: Plan for what happens if you die. Ensure someone you trust knows how to access your crypto (safely, securely).

## Key Takeaways
- Wallets store private keys, not cryptocurrency (which exists on blockchain)
- Hardware wallets are most secure for large amounts
- Never share private keys or seed phrases with anyone
- Use 2FA, strong passwords, and beware of phishing
- Backup seed phrases securely offline
- "Not your keys, not your crypto"—control your own keys for true ownership
